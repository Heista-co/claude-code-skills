# Hook Psychology Reference

Every hook type exploits a specific psychological mechanism. When corpus data is available, use the proven patterns. When it's not, use this reference to generate hooks from the psychology alone.

## 26 Hook Types

### Curiosity Spike — Information Gap Effect
Exploits the information gap effect. The brain can't rest until it closes an open question, so the viewer cannot scroll away. Partial reveals, teased secrets, counterintuitive claims that demand clarification.

**Pattern:** Create a specific piece of missing knowledge the viewer feels compelled to resolve.
**Shell examples:** `{number}% of {audience} don't know {hidden truth}` | `The {adjective} thing about {product} that nobody talks about`

### Open Loop Statement — Zeigarnik Effect
Triggers the Zeigarnik effect. Incomplete thoughts create psychological tension that only watching can resolve. "There's one thing I wish someone told me before I started..."

**Pattern:** Introduce an unfinished thought that creates cognitive debt.
**Shell examples:** `There's one thing about {topic} that changed everything for me` | `I'll show you exactly how, but first...`

### Hidden Truth Reveal — Novelty Bias
The brain assigns higher value to "hidden" information. Information tagged as secret or counter-consensus triggers a dopamine spike that raises perceived value.

**Pattern:** Frame the opening as insider knowledge the viewer isn't supposed to have.
**Shell examples:** `What {industry} doesn't want you to know about {topic}` | `The thing no one tells you about {product}`

### Unexpected Fact Start — Cognitive Dissonance
When a fact clashes with beliefs, the brain demands resolution, locking attention. The viewer can't dismiss the fact without first proving it false.

**Pattern:** Open with a statistic that contradicts what the viewer believes.
**Shell examples:** `{specific number}% of {audience} are doing {common action} wrong` | `The thing you think causes {problem} actually prevents it`

### Contradiction Hook — Prediction Error
Triggers a prediction error. When something sounds wrong, the brain's threat-detection fires, demanding evaluation.

**Pattern:** Open with a statement that deliberately contradicts itself or defies common sense.
**Shell examples:** `The best way to {goal} is to stop {expected action}` | `{Expensive thing} is ruining your {outcome}`

### Provocation — Emotional Salience
Activates the amygdala through emotional charge. Strong reactions override the impulse to scroll. The provocation must connect to the viewer's identity or values.

**Pattern:** Trigger a strong emotional reaction through a personal, specific claim.
**Shell examples:** `Most of you are wasting your {time period} on {common mistake}` | `Your {current approach} is making you {negative outcome}`

### Conflict Statement — Threat Monitoring
The brain is wired to monitor conflicts for survival. Clear stakes between opposing forces demand resolution.

**Pattern:** Introduce an antagonist, problem, or source of friction.
**Shell examples:** `{Authority figure} told me I'd never {goal}` | `Big {industry} companies don't want you to see this`

### Challenge Intro — Goal Tracking
Once a challenge is framed, the viewer needs to see the outcome. Combines curiosity with social proof.

**Pattern:** Frame a specific goal or test the speaker is undertaking.
**Shell examples:** `I tried {method} for {duration} — here's what happened` | `Can you spot the difference between {A} and {B}?`

### Identity Hook — Self-Referential Processing
When the viewer hears their identity described, the message feels instantly personal. Involuntary recognition response lasts 2-3 seconds.

**Pattern:** Name the exact person the ad is for in the first sentence.
**Shell examples:** `If you're a {specific role} who {specific struggle}...` | `For every {identity} who's tried {failed solution}...`

### Tribe Call Out — In-Group Recognition
The brain processes "people like me" signals faster than any other content type. Belonging lowers defenses.

**Pattern:** Name a specific group the viewer belongs to.
**Shell examples:** `Fellow {tribe}, this is for you` | `{Community}, I see you`

### Role Specific Opening — Role-Based Salience
When a viewer's profession is named, they shift into expert mode with higher working memory allocation.

**Pattern:** Name the viewer's job or professional role in the first three seconds.
**Shell examples:** `If you're a {role} running {specific task}...` | `{Professional title}s, listen up`

### Direct Question Hook — Auto-Answer Reflex
The brain auto-generates answers to direct questions, ending passive scrolling. Forces active participation within 500ms.

**Pattern:** Ask a pointed question that demands a personal answer.
**Shell examples:** `Are you {common behavior that hints at problem}?` | `When was the last time you {desired state}?`

### Rhetorical Question — Reflective Processing
Invites reflection without demanding an answer. Creates a private internal dialogue that feels intimate.

**Pattern:** Ask a question you already know the answer to.
**Shell examples:** `Why do the best {product category} never {expected behavior}?` | `Remember when {nostalgic state}?`

### Diagnostic Question — Self-Assessment Instinct
"Am I doing this wrong?" triggers a threat response. The possibility of error creates mild anxiety that locks attention.

**Pattern:** Make the viewer audit their own behavior.
**Shell examples:** `Are you making this {common mistake} with your {activity}?` | `How much of your {resource} are you wasting on {bad approach}?`

### Story Start — Narrative Transport
Once a story begins, the viewer enters the scene. Leaving before the arc resolves feels like abandoning a book mid-chapter.

**Pattern:** Open with a character, a setting, and an implied arc.
**Shell examples:** `{Time marker}, I was {specific situation} when...` | `This is the story of how {ordinary event} changed everything`

### Past Self Open — Empathic Mirroring
Mirror neurons fire when the speaker describes a past struggle, creating instant trust.

**Pattern:** Describe your previous self in vulnerable terms.
**Shell examples:** `{Time period} ago I was {vulnerable state}` | `I used to be the person who {relatable struggle}`

### Flashback Open — Episodic Memory
Temporal displacement forces the brain to build a mental scene, deepening engagement beyond what expository openings achieve.

**Pattern:** Start with a jump to a different time period.
**Shell examples:** `It's {time}. I'm {vivid scene description}.` | `{Time period} ago, everything fell apart`

### Discovery Moment — Social Information Foraging
The brain treats "I just discovered" as time-sensitive and high-value. Freshness creates implicit scarcity.

**Pattern:** Reveal something you just learned.
**Shell examples:** `I just discovered why my {problem} has been so {adjective}` | `I found this {adjective} {product} last {time period}`

### Hypothetical Scenario — Simulation Network
Imagined scenarios are processed as partially real, giving emotional weight. Pre-experiences the product's value.

**Pattern:** Ask the viewer to imagine a specific desired state.
**Shell examples:** `Imagine waking up tomorrow without {pain point}` | `What if you could {desired outcome} without {sacrifice}?`

### Contrast Setup — Anchoring Bias
Two reference points make the brain evaluate comparatively. The anchor makes the preferred option feel dramatically superior.

**Pattern:** Juxtapose two reference points with dramatic difference.
**Shell examples:** `{Expensive option} costs {high price} and takes {long time}. This costs {low price} and takes {short time}` | `{Old way} vs {new way}`

### Process Teaser — Completeness Motivation
Hinting at a method engages the brain's desire for structured knowledge. Methods promise transferable skills.

**Pattern:** Hint at a specific step-by-step method or system.
**Shell examples:** `There's a {number}-step process that changed everything for me` | `I follow this exact formula every {time period}`

### Data Point Start — Anchoring + Authority Bias
Specific numbers create immediate credibility. Precision signals research and bypasses the "this is marketing" filter.

**Pattern:** Open with a specific (not rounded) statistic.
**Shell examples:** `{Specific number}% of {product category} fail this test` | `I tested {specific number} {items} and only {small number} {result}`

### Pattern Observation — Self-Awareness Jolt
Naming an unconscious behavior produces a recognition jolt that locks attention. Feels personal, not salesy.

**Pattern:** Name a behavior the viewer recognizes in themselves.
**Shell examples:** `Have you noticed you always {unconscious behavior}?` | `Every {role} hits this exact wall around {milestone}`

### High Stakes Open — Threat Detection
When consequences feel immediate, the brain prioritizes the message over everything. Use sparingly.

**Pattern:** Communicate immediate consequences in the first three seconds.
**Shell examples:** `If you don't fix this, {serious consequence}` | `This is the mistake that will cost you {valuable thing}`

### Disruptive Statement — Orienting Response
Anything that breaks the scroll pattern captures attention by default. The feed trains pattern-matching; disruption breaks it.

**Pattern:** Declare something that contradicts what an ad sounds like.
**Shell examples:** `I hate {own industry}` | `Most {product category} are a scam`

### Misdirection Open — Prediction Error
Start in one direction, pivot unexpectedly. The pivot forces rapid mental recalculation that costs attention.

**Pattern:** Set up one direction, then reveal the real topic.
**Shell examples:** `This is a video about {topic A}. Actually, it's about why I stopped {topic A}` | `Everyone told me to {expected path}. I did the opposite`

### Prescriptive Cascade — Progressive Self-Identification
Each "If you have X, take Y" repetition raises the chance the viewer matches. Progressive self-qualification through stacked conditionals.

**Pattern:** Stack symptom-to-remedy pairs in a repeating structure.
**Shell examples:** `If you have {symptom 1}, try {remedy 1}. If you have {symptom 2}, try {remedy 2}. If you have {symptom 3}...`

### Parallel List Open — Rule of Three / Completeness Motivation
Two sentences set the pattern, the third completes it. Once the viewer parses the shell, each new sentence slots in effortlessly.

**Pattern:** Stack 3 sentences sharing identical clause structure.
**Shell examples:** `Stop {action 1}. Stop {action 2}. Stop {action 3}.` | `Most people think {X}. Most people think {Y}. Most people think {Z}.`

### Symptom Stack — Recognition Heuristic
Rapid-fire fragments cast a self-qualifying net. Each word is a match-or-skip test at near-instinctive speed.

**Pattern:** Stack single-word or two-word symptom names rapidly.
**Shell examples:** `Tired? Bloated? Brain fog?` | `{Symptom}? {Symptom}? {Symptom}?`
